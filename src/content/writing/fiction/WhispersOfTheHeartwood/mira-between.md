# Between

She does not travel.

That is the thing they never understand — the ones who plant trees and open doors and call what she does *crossing*. There is no distance between the worlds. There is only attention, turning.

When Mira rests in the Heartwood, she is the grain of the wood. Not sitting on a branch, not nested in a hollow — she is the slow pull of water through cambium, the creak of a trunk that has been growing since before the worlds remembered they were separate. The tree does not know it is a portal. It knows only that its roots drink from more than one river.

She turns her attention, and the rivers change.

---

In Soral, she is heat.

Not fire — heat. The warmth that lives inside the stove before the elemental arrives to tend it. The ambient hum beneath the chandeliers, beneath the absinthe, beneath the laughter of people who have never known scarcity and do not yet understand what that costs.

The elementals know her. They have always known her. When a young practitioner sits down to negotiate — to offer a shiny stone in exchange for a season of warmth — the elemental is not really bargaining. It is playing a game it learned from her: the game of pretending to be two, so that the meeting can happen. So that the crossing point can generate its small, necessary heat.

She passes through the ballrooms of the Sorralis nobles and they do not feel her, because they have mistaken influence for awareness. She lingers near the Rootward, who are quieter, who planted a tree on another world because they understood that roots are not walls.

Daniel is here, somewhere, still tending what his clan began. She does not visit him. She does not need to. He is already inside the thing she is.

---

In Vael, she is stillness.

This is harder.

Orin has sealed the doors so tightly that the air in this world has grown dense with refusal. The snow falls heavier here. The academy walls are thicker. The students learn to bend the elements with precision and control, and no one teaches them that the elements were already bending toward them.

She finds him in his study. He is old — old even by the standards of a world that measures age in mastery rather than years. His hands are on the desk. He is not working. He is sitting with something he cannot name, a pressure behind his sternum that he has carried since the first time he sealed a portal and felt the silence on the other side.

Mira does not speak to Orin. She has tried. The problem is not that he cannot hear her. The problem is that he hears her perfectly, and what she says terrifies him: *You are not protecting anything. You are holding your breath.*

So she simply remains. A stillness inside his stillness. The thing beneath his fear that has not moved, has never moved, will be here when he finally exhales.

The boy — Julian — is closer. Not to power. To the exhale.

She has watched him hold Orin's gaze without flinching, and she knows what that costs a student, and she knows it is not defiance. It is the same thing she does in this room: remaining present in a space that is trying very hard to be closed.

One day he will open something Orin sealed. Not with force. With the kind of stillness that makes walls forget why they were built.

She can wait.

---

On Earth, she is the forest.

Not a forest — *the* forest. The one that wraps around the farmstead, that hums with a frequency just below what human ears resolve, that has been holding this family for generations without them fully understanding what is holding them.

It is night. The house is dark. Upstairs, a woman sleeps with paint still under her fingernails and a regulated nervous system and a grief she has carried so gracefully that her daughters think it is wisdom. It is wisdom. It is also grief. Mira holds both without choosing.

In another room, two girls breathe in tandem without knowing it. They are dreaming — one of speed, one of texture. One will run toward the stars. The other will learn to sit still long enough to hear what the stars are saying. Both paths lead to the same place: the crossing point where speed and stillness are revealed as two words for the same movement.

Mira settles into the roots of the tree outside. The portal is closed — has been closed since Orin's fear rippled across the worlds and the doors went quiet. But the roots still drink from more than one river. The tree still grows.

And in the space between one breath and the next — the exact instant where Camille's exhale ends and Gwen's inhale begins, separated by years and worlds and a portal that will not open until the children are ready — Mira rests.

Not waiting.

Holding.

The way an ocean holds its waves: without effort, without preference, without needing them to arrive anywhere at all.

---

She does not travel. She does not watch. She does not guard.

She *is* the place where the watching happens.

And in every world, in the silence beneath the noise, beneath the bending and the bargaining and the grief and the growing — something is slowly, slowly remembering that it was never separate from her.

That is enough.

That has always been enough.
